1. Governance Overview & Intent

Root Map

This page is your index of “everything governance”: which regulations might apply, how you classify data (P0–P3), which frameworks you anchor to, and how that eventually becomes concrete controls in cloud, code, and operations.

Core Pieces

  • Data classification (P0–P3) – how dangerous is exposure?
  • Regulations – HIPAA, PCI DSS, SOX, GLBA, GDPR, FISMA, CJIS, etc.
  • Frameworks – NIST 800-53, NIST CSF, ISO 27001, CIS Controls, SOC 2.
  • Cloud programs – FedRAMP, StateRAMP, IRAP, others.
  • Implementation – Terraform guardrails, Ansible hardening, CI/CD checks.
Data → Regulation → Framework → Controls People · Process · Tech Policy → Standard → Procedure
Idea: You don’t need to memorize everything below. Use this as a lookup table. When someone says “HIPAA app” or “needs FedRAMP High”, you:

1) Classify the data (P2? P3?)
2) Identify regulations that trigger
3) Pick a framework baseline (NIST / ISO / CIS)
4) Map to specific cloud + IaC controls.

2. Governance Decision Flow (High Level)

Flow

Use this flow every time you design a solution or review a proposal. It keeps you from missing a regulation or framework dependency.

Step Question Output
1. Data What data is processed/stored/transmitted? (PHI, cardholder, PII, CJIS, etc.) Data categories (health, payment, identity, criminal, etc.) and P0–P3 impact.
2. Jurisdiction Where are data subjects, orgs, and systems located? Which sectors (public, finance, etc.)? Candidate regulations (HIPAA, PCI DSS, GDPR, FISMA, CJIS, state laws, etc.).
3. Framework What baseline frameworks does your org use? Primary framework(s): NIST 800-53 / NIST CSF / ISO 27001 / CIS Controls / SOC 2.
4. Cloud Program Is there a mandated cloud program (FedRAMP, StateRAMP, etc.)? Target program (e.g., FedRAMP Moderate/High, StateRAMP) and required control set.
5. Control Set Which controls are required across frameworks/programs? Concrete controls: IAM, logging, encryption, config baselines, monitoring.
6. Implementation How do we enforce this in code / cloud? Terraform guardrails, policies, Ansible hardening, CI/CD checks, alerting, runbooks.

This workspace helps with steps 1–5. The Terraform and Ansible workspaces take over at step 6.

3. Data Categories & P0–P3 Impact Levels

Data & Risk

These levels are generic and can be tuned per organization, but they give you a baseline language: “P3 health data” has a very different security bar than “P1 internal metrics”.

Level Typical Data Types Impact if Compromised Typical Controls Baseline
P0 – Public Public marketing content, published documentation, public status pages. Low confidentiality risk; integrity/availability may still matter (defacement, downtime). Hardened hosting, WAF/CDN for public sites, uptime monitoring, backups, change control.
P1 – Internal Only Internal emails, low-sensitivity business docs, internal metrics without PII. Embarrassment, minor financial/operational impact if leaked or modified. IAM with least privilege, MFA for admins, encryption in transit and at rest, basic logging and access reviews.
P2 – Sensitive Customer contact details (PII), non-public financial data, security configs, most application database content. Significant privacy/financial impact, regulatory scrutiny, reputational damage. Strong IAM, segmentation, role-based access, encryption with managed keys, detailed logging/monitoring, change management and approvals.
P3 – Highly Sensitive / Regulated PHI (health records), cardholder data, authentication secrets, keys, law enforcement data (CJI), export-controlled technical data, critical infrastructure controls. Severe impact: regulatory fines, lawsuits, safety risk, national security impact, personal harm. Hardened enclaves or isolated tenants, FIPS-validated crypto where required, zero-trust access, continuous monitoring, dedicated key management, strict SoD (segregation of duties), and tight incident response obligations.

4. Health & Personal Data Regulations

HIPAA & Related

When you’re into medical/health data or personally identifiable information, these are the first names that usually show up.

HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act)

Domain: US healthcare

Purpose

Protect the privacy and security of PHI (Protected Health Information) handled by covered entities (providers, plans, clearinghouses) and their business associates.

Who It Applies To

  • Hospitals, clinics, doctors, health plans, clearinghouses.
  • Vendors (cloud, SaaS, integrators) that create/receive/maintain/transmit PHI.

Scope of Data

PHI: health-related information tied to an identifiable individual (diagnoses, treatment, billing records, etc.). EPHI for electronic forms.

Key Obligations

  • Administrative, physical, and technical safeguards for PHI.
  • Risk analysis and risk management program.
  • Access control, audit logging, integrity protection, transmission security.
  • Business Associate Agreements (BAAs) with vendors handling PHI.
  • Breach notification processes.

Common Pitfalls

  • Shadow IT or apps storing PHI outside defined boundaries.
  • Insufficient logging or inability to produce audit trails for PHI access.
  • Weak vendor management or missing BAAs.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Use PHI-isolated environments (P3 zones) with strict network and IAM controls.
  • Encrypt PHI in transit and at rest; restrict ops access; use key management with separation of duties.
  • Use Terraform to enforce PHI tagging, encryption defaults, logging, and network restrictions.

HITECH (Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health)

Domain: US health IT

Purpose

Strengthens HIPAA, especially for electronic health records (EHR) and breach notification.

Who It Applies To

  • HIPAA-covered entities and business associates handling EHR/EPHI.
  • Health IT systems that digitize or exchange PHI.

Key Obligations

  • Stricter breach notification timelines and penalties.
  • More explicit expectations for EHR security and auditability.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Detailed logging and alerting on EHR platforms.
  • Evidence-friendly configurations (centralized logs, immutable storage, backups).

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

Domain: EU/EEA + global entities processing EU personal data

Purpose

Protect personal data of individuals in the EU/EEA and give them extensive rights over collection, use, access, correction, and deletion of their data.

Who It Applies To

  • Controllers and processors handling personal data of EU/EEA individuals.
  • Applies even if your company is outside the EU but targets or monitors EU residents.

Scope of Data

“Personal data” broadly: any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person (name, email, IP address, identifiers, health data, biometrics, etc.).

Key Obligations

  • Lawful basis for processing (consent, contract, legal obligation, etc.).
  • Transparency and notices; rights to access, rectify, erase, restrict, and port data.
  • Data minimization, purpose limitation, storage limitation.
  • Appropriate security and breach notification obligations.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Data mapping: know where EU personal data lives and flows.
  • Support deletion and export of data per user; retention policies enforced at DB/object-storage level.
  • Terraform/Ansible patterns for per-region isolation, data residency, and retention schedules.

5. Payment & Financial Regulations

PCI, SOX & More

Financial and payment systems carry their own world of expectations: card data, financial reporting integrity, and banking privacy.

PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)

Domain: Global cardholder data security

Purpose

Ensure secure handling of cardholder data (CHD) and sensitive authentication data by merchants, processors, and service providers.

Who It Applies To

  • Any entity that stores, processes, or transmits cardholder data.
  • Service providers that could impact CHD security (hosting, cloud, payment gateways, etc.).

Scope of Data

PAN (primary account number) plus associated data (cardholder name, expiry date, etc.). Sensitive authentication data (CVV, PIN) must never be stored after authorization.

Key Obligations

  • 12 high-level requirements around network security, access, logging, testing, and policy.
  • Strong access control, encryption, and segmentation of the “cardholder data environment” (CDE).
  • Regular vulnerability scans and penetration tests.

Common Pitfalls

  • Letting CHD leak outside the tightly controlled CDE (logs, caches, test data).
  • Inadequate segmentation: entire network ends up in PCI scope.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Design a dedicated, locked-down CDE (network segments, accounts, projects).
  • Use Terraform to enforce segmentation, security groups, WAF, encryption, and logging for CDE resources.
  • Ensure Ansible/ops hardening scripts align with PCI-required configs and patching cadence.

SOX (Sarbanes-Oxley Act)

Domain: US public company financial reporting

Purpose

Improve accuracy and reliability of financial reporting; ensure strong internal controls over financial systems and reporting processes.

Who It Applies To

  • Publicly traded companies in the US.
  • Systems materially supporting financial reporting (GL, ERP, billing, revenue recognition).

Key Obligations

  • Documented internal controls over financial reporting (ICFR).
  • Management assessment and external auditor attestation of control effectiveness.
  • Change management, access control, and segregation of duties for financial systems.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Clearly define which cloud services and databases are “SOX in-scope”.
  • Terraform must enforce consistent access control and logging for in-scope resources.
  • Version-controlled infrastructure and configuration; change approvals and traceability.

GLBA (Gramm–Leach–Bliley Act)

Domain: US financial institutions

Purpose

Protect “non-public personal information” (NPI) collected by financial institutions about their customers.

Who It Applies To

  • Banks, lenders, investment firms, insurance companies, and similar financial institutions.

Key Obligations

  • Safeguards Rule: implement a written information security program.
  • Privacy Rule: inform consumers about data collection and sharing; give opt-out options where required.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Classify and protect NPI as at least P2–P3.
  • Terraform/Ansible hardening and monitoring at the data store and service perimeter.

6. Global Privacy & Data Protection

Privacy

These laws kick in largely based on jurisdiction and who your data subjects are, not just what tech stack you use.

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation)

Domain: EU/EEA and global processing of EU personal data

Key Concepts

  • Controller vs. Processor roles.
  • Lawful bases (consent, contract, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, legitimate interests).
  • Data subject rights (access, erasure, portability, etc.).

Architecture / Cloud Implications

  • Per-region deployments; data residency for EU workloads.
  • APIs and backend workflows that support data subject rights (delete, export).
  • Terraform-managed policies restricting cross-region replication and logs that contain personal data.

CCPA / CPRA (California Consumer Privacy Act / Privacy Rights Act)

Domain: California residents’ personal information

Purpose

Provide consumers with transparency and control over how businesses collect, use, and share their personal information.

Key Rights

  • Right to know what personal information is collected and shared.
  • Right to delete, in many cases.
  • Right to opt out of sale or sharing of personal information.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Data inventory and classification for “personal information”.
  • APIs/workflows for data access and deletion, especially in distributed data stores.
  • Tagging and access controls to limit which systems hold data subject to CCPA/CPRA.

Other National / State Privacy Laws

Many jurisdictions (Brazil LGPD, Canada PIPEDA, other US states) are adding GDPR-style requirements. Use the same core pattern:

  • Maintain a unified data inventory and data flow diagrams.
  • Standardize technical controls via your chosen frameworks (NIST, ISO, CIS).
  • Use Terraform/Ansible to apply the same baseline across multiple regions and providers.

7. Public Sector, Defense & Criminal Justice

Gov / Justice

When you’re dealing with government systems, classified data, or criminal justice information, these programs and standards show up quickly.

FISMA (Federal Information Security Modernization Act)

Domain: US federal information systems

Purpose

Require federal agencies and some contractors to implement information security programs based on NIST standards (e.g., NIST SP 800-53).

Key Elements

  • System categorization (Low, Moderate, High) based on impact.
  • Selection and tailoring of NIST 800-53 controls.
  • Continuous monitoring and authorization (A&A) processes.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Use NIST 800-53 as main control catalog for design and implementation.
  • Map Terraform modules and policies directly to control families (AC, AU, SC, etc.).
  • Enable continuous monitoring hooks (SIEM, CSPM tools, etc.).

FIPS (Federal Information Processing Standards)

Domain: US government cryptographic and security requirements

Key Concept

FIPS 140-2 / 140-3 sets requirements for cryptographic modules. When required, crypto components must be FIPS-validated.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Use FIPS-validated crypto libraries and cloud key management systems when mandates apply.
  • Terraform variables and modules that enforce “FIPS-only” modes or approved key types/algorithms.

CJIS (Criminal Justice Information Services)

Domain: US law enforcement / criminal justice information (CJI)

Purpose

Protect CJI held by law enforcement, courts, and related agencies or partners.

Key Obligations

  • Detailed policy covering access control, authentication, encryption, logging, training, and more.
  • Personnel screening and background checks for access to CJI.
  • Physical security requirements for facilities and data centers.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Dedicated CJIS-aligned environments or cloud regions where supported.
  • Strong IAM policies, just-in-time access, and auditable logging of all CJI access.
  • Terraform-managed segmentation and encryption; Ansible hardening for CJI workloads.

ITAR / EAR

Domain: Export-controlled defense & dual-use technical data

Purpose

Control export and handling of technical data related to defense articles (ITAR) and certain dual-use items (EAR).

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Region and personnel restrictions (only certain citizenships and geographies).
  • Sovereign cloud and strong data residency controls.
  • Terraform-based guardrails that prevent deployment in non-approved regions.

8. Security Frameworks & Control Catalogs

Frameworks

Frameworks are the “control language” you use to structure your governance program. Regulations tell you what you must achieve; frameworks give you the catalog of controls to do it systematically.

NIST SP 800-53

Type: Detailed control catalog

Purpose

Provide a comprehensive list of security and privacy controls, organized into families (AC – Access Control, AU – Audit, SC – System & Communications, etc.).

Why It Matters

  • Backbone of FISMA, FedRAMP, and many public-sector security programs.
  • Often reused in enterprises as a core control set even outside government.

Architecture / Cloud Use

  • Map each Terraform module/Ansible role to relevant control families.
  • Create “control coverage” views: which stacks implement which controls.

NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

Type: High-level framework (Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover)

Use

  • Gives executives and architects a simple, shared language for security posture.
  • Maps to more detailed frameworks like 800-53 or ISO controls underneath.

ISO/IEC 27001 (& Related Standards)

Type: ISMS (Information Security Management System) standard

ISO 27001

  • Defines requirements for an ISMS and Annex A control set.
  • Widely used globally; certification is often requested by customers.

Related

  • 27002 – Control implementation guidance.
  • 27017 – Cloud-specific security controls.
  • 27018 – Protection of PII in public cloud.
  • 27701 – Privacy extension (PII Controllers/Processors).

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Align your baseline hardening and access control to ISO Annex A / 27017 / 27018.
  • Use Terraform and Ansible baselines as control implementation evidence for audits.

CIS Controls

Type: Prescriptive control set

Prioritized “top N” security actions: inventory, secure configuration, vulnerability management, logging, etc. Good for day-to-day security engineering.

  • Map directly into Terraform/Ansible hardening and baseline patterns.
  • Great starting point for practical control implementation.

COBIT

Type: IT governance framework

Focuses on aligning IT processes and controls with business goals and governance. Often used by auditors and governance teams.

MITRE ATT&CK

Type: Adversary tactics & techniques matrix

Not a control catalog but a model of attacker behavior. Used heavily for detection engineering, threat hunting, and incident response.

  • Map detections and logging patterns to ATT&CK techniques.
  • Ensure Terraform/Ansible deploy log sources and telemetry needed for those detections.

Answering your original question directly: yes, you want frameworks on top of raw regulations. They give you a structured, reusable control language for Terraform, Ansible, and audits, instead of ad-hoc one-off controls.

9. SOC 2 & Trust Service Criteria

Assurance

SOC 2 is an attestation framework used heavily in SaaS and service providers. It focuses on “Trust Services Criteria” (TSC) rather than prescribing specific controls.

Trust Services Category What It Focuses On Examples of Technical Themes
Security Protection against unauthorized access Access control, network security, logging, vulnerability management, change control.
Availability Service uptime and performance Redundancy, failover, capacity management, backup and restore, DR testing.
Confidentiality Protection of confidential information Data encryption, segregation, DLP, restricted access to secrets and code.
Processing Integrity Complete, valid, accurate, timely processing Input validation, transaction controls, reconciling jobs, robust SDLC.
Privacy Personal information handling practices Consent, data subject rights processes, retention and deletion controls.

SOC 2 doesn’t tell you “use this cipher” or “turn on this specific log”. Instead, you design controls using NIST/ISO/CIS, then map them into the SOC 2 Trust Services Criteria; auditors evaluate design and operating effectiveness.

10. Cloud Programs (FedRAMP, StateRAMP, etc.)

Cloud

These programs sit on top of NIST/FIPS and define what’s required for cloud services offered to particular government sectors.

FedRAMP (Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program)

Audience: US federal agencies consuming cloud services

Purpose

Standardize security assessment, authorization, and continuous monitoring for cloud products used by US federal agencies.

Key Elements

  • NIST 800-53-based baselines (Low, Moderate, High).
  • Authorization to Operate (ATO) from an agency or the JAB.
  • Continuous monitoring and reporting requirements.

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Design FedRAMP “stack” with strict boundaries and audited controls.
  • Terraform is used to codify the entire authorized boundary and its controls.

StateRAMP & Other National Programs

StateRAMP: US state/local government oriented, modeled after FedRAMP.

Purpose

Give state and local governments a consistent way to assess the security of cloud services.

Other Programs

  • IRAP (Australia), and other country-specific sovereign cloud programs.
  • Typically map to a national control baseline (NIST-like or ISO-based).

Architecture / Cloud Impact

  • Separate tenants, regions, or accounts for each program where needed.
  • Baseline patterns and Terraform modules per program (FedRAMP, StateRAMP, IRAP, etc.).

11. Requirement → Regulations → Frameworks

Decision Map

This table is the “cheat sheet” for common patterns. Start from a sentence someone says in a meeting and chase it across data, regs, and frameworks.

Requirement Pattern Data / P Level Likely Regulations Frameworks / Programs
“We’re building a cloud-hosted electronic health records system.” P3, PHI, health data HIPAA, HITECH, possibly GDPR/other privacy laws if global NIST 800-53 or ISO 27001 baseline; HIPAA mappings; FedRAMP if federal
“We store and process credit card payments online.” P3, cardholder data PCI DSS, possibly privacy laws (GDPR, CCPA, etc.) PCI DSS mapped to NIST/ISO/CIS; SOC 2 Security/Availability for assurance
“SaaS product for public sector agencies in the US.” P2–P3, depending on agency and data FISMA context, plus sector laws (health, justice, etc.) FedRAMP baseline; NIST 800-53; FIPS crypto where required
“Analytics platform with EU customer data.” P2–P3 (PII, possibly sensitive) GDPR, maybe other national privacy laws NIST CSF or ISO 27001/27701; SOC 2 Privacy/Confidentiality
“Law enforcement case management for multiple states.” P3, CJI CJIS; state laws; maybe FISMA if federal NIST 800-53 with CJIS overlays; possibly StateRAMP; strong logging/encryption
Start with data and who you serve Then pick regulations and frameworks Then enforce via Terraform / Ansible baselines

12. Implementation Hooks (Terraform, Ansible, Pipelines)

Execution

This page stops at the governance map. Terraform, Ansible, and automation are where you turn the map into guardrails and concrete controls.

Terraform Hooks

  • Landing zone modules – enforce segmentation, baseline logging, and encryption by default for P2/P3 patterns.
  • Policy-as-code – validate configs against NIST/ISO/CIS mappings (e.g., OPA, Sentinel, ConfTest).
  • Tagging & metadata – encode classification (P0–P3), owner, data type, and regulation tags onto resources.

Ansible / Ops Hooks

  • Hardening roles – CIS/NIST/ISO-aligned OS and middleware baselines for P2/P3 systems.
  • Audit & logging roles – ensure logs needed for HIPAA/PCI/FedRAMP/CJIS are consistently enabled and shipped.
  • Runbooks & response – incident response and change management tied back to NIST CSF (Respond/Recover).

This workspace is deliberately implementation-neutral. The Terraform and Ansible HTML workspaces carry the actual code patterns; here you define what must exist so those patterns have something to anchor to.
